Are You Helping or Enabling Your Adult Child?

Are You Helping or Enabling Your Adult Child?

4 crucial strategies for helping your child without losing yourself in the process.

Key points

  • Rescuing too much can prevent growth, while healthy support builds resilience.

  • Support doesn’t have to mean solving every problem.

  • As a parent, it’s easy to put your child’s needs above your own.

As a parent coach, I’ve witnessed the challenges parents face when their adult children are struggling. The line between helping and enabling can be slim. I have seen many well-meaning parents throw on their super parent cape and fly off in an attempt to ease their child’s pain. But rescuing too much can prevent growth, while healthy support builds resilience.

Let’s explore two real-life scenarios that illustrate this delicate balance.

The Overextended Supporter

Leslie’s son, Jasper, at 26, had a history of quitting jobs after a few months. His reasons varied—“toxic environments,” “unfair management”—but Leslie’s solution was always the same: Pay his rent and let him regroup. Jasper’s career ambitions, however, remained stagnant, and Leslie felt like she was drowning in guilt when she didn’t help.

Leslie’s love for Jasper was evident, but she inadvertently enabled his lack of direction. By continuing to offer financial support without clear expectations, she prevented him from facing the consequences and taking responsibility for his future.

Tip: Leslie could have helped Jasper create a realistic budget and job-search plan instead of paying rent. As I describe in my book 10 Days to a Less Defiant Child, providing emotional support and practical tools to struggling adult children empowers them to take ownership of their situation.

The Tough-Love Parent

Greg’s daughter Marnie, 29, was an artist with dreams of opening a studio. Greg had invested heavily in her vision, giving her a lump sum to fund her business. However, Marnie spent more time on social media than on actual work, and the company was going nowhere fast.

Greg was frustrated. He loved his daughter but realized that his financial support wasn’t having the intended effect—it was enabling her procrastination. After a tough conversation, Greg offered a different kind of help: He would match any funds she saved but wouldn’t give her another penny unless she showed real effort.

Tip: Greg’s approach allowed Marnie to take ownership of her business, setting boundaries that encouraged her to work harder and be more responsible.

How to Help Without Enabling

  1. Define What Support Looks Like. Support doesn’t have to mean solving every problem. For Leslie, this meant shifting from paying Jasper’s rent to helping him create a budget and explore job opportunities. Instead of footing the bill, she offered emotional encouragement and practical tools, empowering him to take ownership of his situation.

    Tip: When your child asks for help, ask yourself: Am I doing this to alleviate their struggle or my discomfort? If it’s the latter, consider a different approach.

  2. Stop Feeling Guilty About Saying No It’s OK to say no when your financial, emotional, or other resources are depleted. Greg learned this the hard way. He set a boundary with Marnie, offering to match her savings dollar-for-dollar instead of funding her outright. At first, she bristled, but over time, she began to take her goals more seriously.

    Tip: Frame your “no” to say “yes” to their growth. For example, “I can’t fund this project, but I believe in you and am happy to brainstorm ways you can make it work.”

  3. Respect Their Independence (Even If It’s Messy). Sometimes, watching your child struggle is more complicated than stepping in. But growth often comes from trial and error. It wasn't glamorous when Jasper finally found a job, but it gave him a sense of pride. Leslie realized that her stepping back allowed him to step up.

    Tip: Resist the urge to micromanage. Instead, offer unconditional support: “I’m here for you, no matter what.

  4. Take Care of Yourself. Putting your child’s needs above your own is easy as a parent. But when you’re running on empty, you’re less effective at providing the kind of support they truly need.

    Tip: Prioritize activities and relationships that replenish you. Remember, modeling self-care is one of the most valuable lessons you can offer your child.

Final Thoughts

Parenting doesn’t stop when your child turns 18; it evolves. When you set boundaries, you’re not withholding love but redefining it. You’re teaching your child to navigate challenges while preserving your well-being.
